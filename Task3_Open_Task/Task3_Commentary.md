Quantum computing is a relatively new field in computing that uses the principles of quantum mechanics to solve problems that are too complex for classical computers, such as prime factorization and the traveling salesman problem. The principles of quantum mechanics have fascinated and puzzled some of the greatest scientific minds. As Richard Feynman once said, “If you think you understand quantum mechanics, you don’t understand quantum mechanics.” This highlights both the mystery and depth of the field.
Quantum computing changes how we process information. Classical computers use bits (0s and 1s) which are fixed and deterministic — what I like to call static. In contrast, quantum computers use qubits, which exist in superposition and are probabilistic — what I would describe as active. To me, quantum computing feels less like a faster computer and more like a fundamentally different, and possibly better, way of solving problems.
However, despite its promise, quantum computing still faces major challenges. Current hardware is scarce, expensive, and difficult to maintain due to high energy demands. There are also technical issues like noise and crosstalk, which introduce errors and reduce reliability in quantum computations.
I am relatively new to the field, but I have worked with Cirq and TensorFlow Quantum. These tools make it easier to build hybrid models that combine classical and quantum approaches, which I believe is the most practical direction given current hardware limitations. I am currently applying this knowledge to build a quantum machine learning model for agriculture, leveraging my practical experience in that domain.
I am also interested in the application of quantum computing in security and cryptography. The field is rapidly evolving from theory to real-world deployment, especially with the rise of Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC), which is now seeing standardization and increasing adoption by governments.
In conclusion, I believe quantum computing and quantum machine learning have immense potential. As an upcoming computer scientist, I feel a responsibility to contribute to its development in a way that is ethical and beneficial to society.
